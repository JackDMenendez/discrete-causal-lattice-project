## Summary
Addendum to `2026-07-18-paper04-falsifiability-do-both-decision`. Carries the
author's rebuttal to the common dismissal ("no evidence of birefringence, so the
DCL says nothing / is unfalsifiable"). The point: a predicted **null** on a
tightly-bounded, observable channel is a **falsifiable prediction already passed**
— and the same non-observation that is cited against discrete-spacetime models
is exactly what **excludes the birefringent (dim-5) rivals** while **corroborating**
the DCL. Fold it into the falsifiability section as a strength; keep the two
honesty guardrails so it cannot be re-inverted.

## The argument (for the section)
1. **Falsifiable = forbids an observable outcome** (Popper). The DCL forbids
   optical-axis birefringence. Observe it → theory dead. It was not observed →
   corroborated risky prediction.
2. **The dismissal inverts this** — it reads "predicts a null" as "says nothing."
   But a null on a channel where the default expectation is a signal, and the
   signal is excluded, is a strong prediction, not silence.
3. **It discriminates in our favor.** Generic discrete/LV/QG models predict
   vacuum birefringence (dim-5, CPT-odd, helicity-dependent); the astrophysical
   bounds exclude them. The DCL predicts the cancellation and survives.
4. **It is structural, not luck.** Birefringence is odd-dimension (dim-5);
   the DCL sits in the even-dimension (dim-6), non-birefringent photon class,
   which instead gives direction/energy-dependent dispersion. The null is what
   the class requires; the dim-6 dispersion (2.a/2.b) is where the real test is.
